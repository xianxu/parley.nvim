This is a really common and underappreciated problem. The business leader thinks in outcomes, markets, and timelines; the technical leader thinks in systems, constraints, and dependencies. You're essentially acting as a **translation layer**, which is valuable but fragile if it lives only in your head.

### On showing "size and complexity" in your dependency graph

Mermaid is great for structure but weak for encoding **magnitude**. What you want is a graph where nodes carry visual weight. A few options:

**Option A: Weighted node graphs (best fit)**

- **D3.js force-directed graphs** — nodes can be sized by complexity (e.g., story points, estimated weeks, number of unknowns) and colored by domain (business vs. tech). Edges show dependencies. This is the most expressive option but requires some coding.
- **[🌿:Graphviz (DOT language)](2026-04-03.07-16-59.100.md)** — supports node sizing, shapes, colors, and cluster groupings. Less interactive but very precise. You can generate `.dot` files programmatically.

**Option B: Treemaps / Bubble charts**

- If hierarchy matters more than dependency, a **treemap** (e.g., in Notion charts, Observable, or even Google Sheets) encodes size as area. Good for showing "this technical initiative is 5x the effort of that one."

**Option C: Wardley Maps**

This might be the missing piece for you. Wardley Maps were designed *exactly* for the problem you're describing:

- **X-axis**: evolution stage (genesis → custom → product → commodity)
- **Y-axis**: visibility to the user/business
- Components are placed on the map and connected by dependency lines
- You can annotate with effort/complexity

It forces business and technical leaders to look at the **same map** and argue about positioning rather than talking past each other. It's specifically designed to bridge strategy and technology.

**Option D: Impact/Effort matrices with dependency overlays**

A 2x2 (impact vs. effort) where you draw dependency arrows between items. Simple, but surprisingly effective in meetings. Tools like Miro or FigJam work well here.

### Practical suggestion

For your specific situation — dual-track vision with dependencies and complexity — I'd recommend:

1. **Wardley Map** for the strategic alignment conversation (quarterly)
2. **Weighted dependency graph** (D3 or Graphviz) for the operational/planning view
3. Keep your Mermaid graphs for documentation — they're version-controllable and readable in PRs

### On the broader alignment problem

A few hard-won patterns:

- **Shared artifact, not shared language**: Don't try to make the business leader speak tech or vice versa. Create a single visual artifact (like the Wardley Map) that both can point at and disagree on. The artifact *is* the translation layer, not you.
- **Name the bets**: Every long-term vision item is a bet. Frame them as "We believe [X] will lead to [Y], and we'll know by [Z]." This works in both languages.
- **Stagger intentionally**: Business vision should lead by ~1 quarter. Technical vision responds with "here's what we need to build/change to enable that." The dependency graph you're building is exactly the right connector.
