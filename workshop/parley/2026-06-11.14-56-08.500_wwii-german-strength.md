Your instinct points at something real, but the framing "out of nowhere" is the part I'd push back on first — and the "military innovation" thesis is only partly right. Let me lay out why.

### Germany didn't appear from nowhere

Before WWI, Germany was already the strongest land power in continental Europe and a top-tier industrial economy. The defeat in 1918 and the Treaty of Versailles *suppressed* that power — capping the army at 100,000 men, banning tanks, an air force, and a general staff — but it didn't erase the underlying capacity: the factories, engineers, trained officer corps, and institutional knowledge remained. So 1939 is better understood as a *re-emergence* of latent strength than a sudden rise.

### The innovation thesis: partly true, but not the way most people think

The popular image is that Germany won early through superior technology. This is largely a **myth**:

- German tanks in 1939–40 were often *inferior* to French and British ones in armor and gun caliber.
- Most of the Wehrmacht was **not** mechanized — it moved on foot and relied on horses for transport throughout the entire war (millions of horses).
- The radio, the tank, the dive bomber — none were uniquely German inventions.

What was genuinely innovative was **doctrine and integration**, not hardware:

- **Combined arms / Blitzkrieg**: concentrating tanks, motorized infantry, artillery, and close air support to punch through a narrow point and disrupt the enemy's rear and command, rather than grinding along a broad front.
- **Decentralized command** (*Auftragstaktik*): junior officers were trained to take initiative toward a commander's intent rather than wait for orders, giving German forces a tempo advantage.
- **Radio in every tank**, enabling coordination that the technically-comparable French armor lacked.

### The context that mattered as much as doctrine

- **Versailles paradox**: a small army forced to be highly professional became a high-quality cadre to expand from. Secret cooperation with the USSR (1920s) let Germany test tanks and aircraft abroad.
- **Rapid rearmament** under the Nazis from 1935, deficit-financed and aggressive.
- **Timing and surprise**: opponents were unprepared, politically divided (appeasement), and stuck with WWI-era defensive thinking (e.g., the Maginot Line).

### The critical caveat — the strength was partly an illusion

This is where I'd be most skeptical of the "Germany was overwhelmingly strong" narrative. Early victories against Poland and France were dramatic but came against specific weaknesses. Germany's economy was **not** fully mobilized for total war until 1942–43, and its strength was **front-loaded**: well-suited to short, sharp campaigns but unable to sustain a long war against the combined industrial output of the USSR, the USA, and the British Empire. Once the war became attritional, the early "invincibility" evaporated.

So: the answer is **not primarily innovation in technology**, but the integration of existing tools into a superior operational doctrine, layered on a pre-existing industrial base, exploited against unprepared opponents — and even then the strength was more fragile than it looked.
