There are two layers to this: a simple statistical link and the deeper physical mechanisms. The statistical one is necessary but, on its own, slightly misleading, so it's worth separating them.

### 1. The statistical link: shifting the distribution

Heat waves are, by definition, extremes in the tail of the temperature distribution. Global warming raises the **mean** of that distribution. Even if the *shape* of the distribution stayed identical and you just slid the whole bell curve to the right by a degree or two, the area in the hot tail grows **disproportionately**. A small shift in the mean produces a large multiplication in the frequency of extremes.

So even a modest rise in average temperature makes record-breaking heat much more likely. This is the cleanest, most defensible version of the link.

### 2. Why it's more than just a shifted mean

The distribution doesn't only slide — its **shape changes** too. In many regions the variance increases and the hot tail stretches, so extremes warm *faster* than the mean. This is driven by physical feedbacks, not just statistics:

- **Soil-moisture / evaporation feedback.** Normally, when the ground is moist, incoming solar energy goes partly into evaporating water (latent heat) rather than heating the air (sensible heat). During a dry spell the soil dries out, evaporation shuts down, and nearly all that energy goes into raising air temperature. Warming makes soils dry out more readily, so this feedback amplifies heat over land. This is a major reason land heats faster than the global average.

- **Atmospheric circulation / blocking.** Heat waves usually require a persistent high-pressure "blocking" pattern that parks clear skies over a region for days. There's an active and *unsettled* debate about whether warming (especially Arctic amplification weakening the jet stream) makes such blocking more frequent or persistent. I'd flag this as genuinely contested — less certain than the soil-moisture mechanism.

### 3. Humidity matters as much as temperature

Warmer air holds more moisture (roughly 7% more per °C, from Clausius–Clapeyron). Human heat stress depends on **wet-bulb temperature**, which combines heat and humidity, because we cool by sweating. So warming raises not just dry temperatures but the humid-heat extremes that are physiologically far more dangerous.

### 4. A caution on attribution

It's worth being precise: warming doesn't "cause" any single heat wave — weather provides the event, climate changes the **odds**. The rigorous framing is probabilistic (e.g., "this event was made N times more likely"), which is what attribution science actually quantifies.
